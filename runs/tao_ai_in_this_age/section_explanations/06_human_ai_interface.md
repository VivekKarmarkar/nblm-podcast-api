# Section 6 — The human/AI interface (pages 20-23)

## Load-bearing point

This section gives the essay's ACTIONABLE proposal: a three-tier framework for thinking about how to coexist with AI, evolving on a short / medium / long time horizon. The third tier ends in a beautiful and original metaphor — a "Copernican view" of intelligence.

## Four sub-pieces

**6.1 Short term: AI as the "vanilla extract" of intellectual production.** A culinary analogy. Vanilla extract, by itself, is "extremely unpleasant" to drink. But in small amounts, it enhances and improves the flavors of nearly any sweet dish, even when it cannot be differentiated from them. Most people understand there's an upper limit beyond which it ruins the dish entirely (footnote: a Tumblr thought experiment concluded a cake that was 44% vanilla extract would be inedible). Most of us don't know precisely where that limit is, so we keep it as "a very minor addition."

Same with current AI usage: an optional addition to cognitive workflows — passing a human-composed text through an AI for grammar suggestions, handing a list of bullet points to an AI to organize. "These light touches, like a small splash of vanilla, will enhance and enrich the character of the work without overwhelming it. AI-content that is utilized as the core components of such workflows, however, will not yield desirable, effective, or valuable outcomes."

**6.2 Medium term: AI on the "red team."** As AI tools increase in capability and adoption, the ability to "opt out" diminishes. Even individuals who personally avoid AI will work with colleagues, students, institutions that increasingly use it. The terminology comes from cybersecurity: a "blue team" defends a system, a "red team" probes it for weaknesses.

Mapping this onto intellectual work: "blue team" tasks are generating new content and structures; "red team" tasks are verifying, testing, maintaining that content. AI is "relatively safe to utilize in a red team capacity of reviewing human-generated content for errors or suggested improvements." But — given "the stochastic unreliability and lack of groundedness of the current and near-term tools" — it is unsafe to trust AI in any "blue team structural capacity that is beyond the ability of the red team (which could consist of humans or more automated verification tools, such as formal proof assistants) to verify."

In this philosophy, the emphasis is on managing AI risk while still capturing many of its benefits — rather than radically rethinking the fundamental nature of the field.

**6.3 Longer term: is a philosophical retreat inevitable?** Suppose a more distant future in which AI's current weaknesses are resolved and capabilities match or exceed expert humans across the board. (Sometimes called Artificial General Intelligence, with no consensus on the term.) How do we respond to the philosophical questions then?

Three retreat positions, all of which the authors view as inadequate:
1. **Pure formalism / "shut up and calculate."** In math, the formalist view: the only objective is manipulating mathematical symbols according to precise rules. In science: don't worry about philosophy, just compute. In art: work as an artisan, fulfilling external specifications, without judging the value of the product. No distinction need be made between human-generated and AI-generated work, so long as technical specifications are met. But this "does not capture the full experience of how mathematics, science, and the arts are conducted in practice, and provides little guidance on such practical questions as how to motivate the next generation of students, or what directions of curiosity-driven research to pursue."
2. **Ineffable human soul / "No True Scotsman."** Ascribe an ineffable special status to human intellect or human creativity. AI is forever lacking true "soul" or "understanding." But humans themselves are "spiky" in their abilities, "sometimes lucking into successfully achieving a task through random word association and rote memorization" — yet we judge AI more harshly for these same behaviors. This position risks degenerating into a "god of the gaps" philosophy with an ever-shrinking list of qualities supposedly indicating essential human achievement.
3. **AI supersedes everything.** All human cognitive abilities will be superseded; philosophical discussions about human value become moot. In extreme versions, "the very exercise of human intellect is viewed as an undesirable and tedious activity, which ought to be replaced by automation as quickly as possible." Footnote: "for a vision of what this framework would look like in practice, we suggest the science-fiction film Wall-E."

A philosophical middle ground exists. Precedent: chess. It has been decades since any human grandmaster beat a chess engine. Yet chess remains "a popular and thriving human activity, with chess players incorporating engines into their training, using them to revisit old chess theories and explore new ones, probe for exploits and weaknesses in otherwise invincible AI chess players, or creatively introduce new forms of competition that involve varying levels of AI assistance." The philosophical questions of what chess is and what value is in playing it continue to matter — and the accepted answers don't closely match any of the three extreme positions.

**6.4 A Copernican view.** This is the essay's most original philosophical move. In antiquity, the dominant view of the cosmos was geocentric — Earth at the immobile center of the universe. Successive scientific advances dismantled that: Earth rotates, orbits the Sun, the Sun orbits the galactic center, the galaxy is part of an expanding universe with no spatial center. This "Copernican principle" — Earth is just one planet among countless others — felt threatening at first to humanity's emotional attachment to our home planet. But there's no fundamental contradiction between the universe's disinterest in Earth and our own strong investment in it. "We can still quite justifiably prioritize issues specific to planet Earth over those on other planets, while simultaneously accepting that these other planets exist and would be of comparable importance to their own inhabitants." Similar revolutions: Darwin dethroning humans among species; the dethroning of Euclidean geometry as a unique source of geometric truth.

Until recently we have embraced "an intellectual analogue of the geocentric model" — human intelligence at the center of the cognitive universe, with special philosophical status. But "now we are discovering (or creating) other planets of intelligence comparable in many ways to our own, while simultaneously being quite distinct in many aspects." The proposal: accept that both human and artificial intelligences "exist in the same ontological category, though with many distinctive differences and complementarities." Our interests will still be largely tied to the human intellectual sphere, but its relationship with other forms of intelligence can be explored — for practical purposes AND for achieving "an external perspective on human cognition that was previously difficult to attain."

## What makes this section narratively brilliant

The vanilla-extract analogy is FUNNY (44% vanilla cake) and immediately intuitive. The blue-team / red-team metaphor lands cleanly — AI as a CRITIC of human work is safe, AI as a CREATOR of new content is risky. The chess precedent is concrete and lived. And the Copernican framing is genuinely elegant — it offers a way to make peace with AI's existence that does not require either capitulation OR a "soul" mysticism. For audio this is the second-most-quotable section after Section 4.
