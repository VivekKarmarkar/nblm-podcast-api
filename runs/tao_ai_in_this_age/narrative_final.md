Picture this. Two writers sit down to compose an essay. It's an essay about artificial intelligence. About what AI is doing to thought, to mathematics, to the practice of careful work. Big subject. Important. They open their laptops. They start typing.

And the AI in their word processor starts writing the essay for them.

Not once. Three times. Three different AI agents, on three different occasions, inserted themselves into the draft unsolicited. The writers manually pulled each contribution back out and kept going. There's a footnote in the final paper that just says, dryly: any and all of these AI contributions were promptly removed from the text.

That's how the paper begins. It's a small detail. It's the perfect detail. Because the entire paper is about exactly this: the experience of trying to do serious cognitive work in a world where machines keep trying, with varying degrees of competence and consent, to do that work alongside you.

The two writers are Tanya Klowden and Terence Tao. Tanya studies art. Terence Tao is, by wide consensus, one of the most decorated living mathematicians — the kind of person who wins fields medals and cracks problems mathematicians have stared at for generations. Disciplines that look like opposites. But both of them use AI tools daily. Both have ended up thinking very hard about what those tools are doing to their work. And both have noticed, to their surprise, that the messy human-scale questions are the same in art and in mathematics.

So they wrote this paper together. And here's the thing they want you to understand. They are not telling you whether AI is good or bad. They are telling you what it's doing to actual cognitive work, right now, from the inside.

OK. Why is a mathematician the right person to listen to about this?

The argument goes like this. Almost every field is being changed by AI, but most fields are messy. The boundaries of good work are fuzzy. Disagreements drag on. Mathematics is different. Mathematics has, since Euclid, this unusual property: when two mathematicians disagree about whether a proof is correct, they can usually settle it. They can write the steps out in fine enough detail that you can mechanically check each one. There's a court of appeal that actually works.

That makes math a clean laboratory. If you want to know what AI does when it tries to do real intellectual work, math is the place to look first, because in math you can actually CHECK whether the AI got it right. If something breaks in math, you'll see it. If something doesn't break in math, you might be on to something.

And the headline finding is this. AI can now solve hard math problems. Olympiad-level hard, sometimes research-level hard. It produces proofs. The proofs are correct — you can run them through a verifier and confirm, mechanically, that each step follows. So in some narrow sense, the AI is doing mathematics.

But here's the strange part. The way the AI gets to the proof bears almost no resemblance to the way a human mathematician would. A human, faced with a hard problem, tries small examples. Plays around. Builds intuition. Notices patterns. Conjectures something general. Tries to break the conjecture. Tries to prove it. The proof, when it comes, is the END of a long process of UNDERSTANDING.

The AI does none of that. It just produces a proof. The proof is right. The understanding never happens.

Tao has a name for this. He calls it a decoupling. A decoupling between the ABILITY to prove a theorem and the REASONING processes you'd need to actually understand it. And once you've heard him say it that way, you start seeing this decoupling everywhere. Not just in math. In writing, in art, in code. The product comes out polished. The thought process that used to create the product has been... skipped. Bypassed. Outsourced.

What do we do with that?

Here's where the paper gets really lovely. To answer that question, Tao reaches for a sensory metaphor. He calls it the smell test.

The smell test is what experienced mathematicians do without realizing they're doing it. You open a paper. You glance at the structure of the argument. You notice the choice of notation, the framing of the lemmas, the order in which the difficulties are addressed. And before you've checked a single line carefully, you have a feeling. A nose for it. This proof smells right. Or, more often: this proof smells off.

It's not magic. It's pattern recognition built up over years. There's a famous blog post by Scott Aaronson called Ten signs a claimed mathematical breakthrough is wrong, and Tao reaches for it. The signs are things like: the proof doesn't engage with the parts of the problem that are obviously hard. Or the proof claims to use machinery that doesn't quite fit. Or the proof doesn't tell you why anything works, just that it does.

That last one matters most. Because a good mathematical proof, the kind that gets celebrated in the field, doesn't just verify a result. It tells you a story. It tells you which parts of the argument are doing the heavy lifting. Which parts are routine. Which moves are new. Which connections to other parts of math you should be holding in your head. The proof carries inside it the seeds of how you'd generalize it, how you'd find related results, how you'd teach it to someone who didn't know the field.

That kind of proof has a good smell. It's nourishing. You can build on it.

And here's the worry. AI-generated proofs can pass formal verification — they can be mechanically certified as correct — and still have no smell at all. They have the right notation, the right structure, the right look. But they don't tell you a story. They don't show you the load-bearing beams of the argument. They just establish the result.

A concrete example. In 2024, an AI system called AlphaProof generated solutions to problems from the International Mathematical Olympiad. The solutions were formally verified to be correct. They also contained, depending on how you count, numerous redundant or inexplicable steps. The proofs work. Nobody can fully explain why those particular steps are in them.

In a world where proofs are mass-produced this way, something gets lost. Not correctness. Insight. The thing the proof was for. The thing math was for.

OK, take that in for a second. One of the most accomplished living mathematicians is telling you that the AI is getting the right answers and missing the entire point.

But — and this is where the paper saves you from sliding into despair — he doesn't think we're stuck. He thinks there's a way through. The whole second half of the paper is about figuring out what that way looks like.

He proposes thinking about it in three time horizons. Right now. Soon. Eventually.

Right now, the proposal is this. Treat AI like vanilla extract.

Yes. Vanilla extract. The cooking ingredient.

Think about how you use it. You don't drink it. Drunk by itself it tastes terrible. But you add a tiny splash to a cake, and the cake gets better. You add it to cookies, the cookies get better. The vanilla doesn't even register as a distinct flavor — it just makes the rest of the dish bloom. Most cooks know there's some upper limit beyond which more vanilla ruins the dish, but most of us couldn't tell you exactly where that limit is. So we keep it small. We err on the side of just a touch.

That's how to use AI right now. Hand it a sentence and ask it to polish the grammar. Hand it a list of bullet points and ask it to suggest a structure. Small touches. Enhancing the human-led work, not replacing it. The cake is still your cake. The vanilla just helps it sing.

There's a hilarious footnote in the paper here. Somebody on Tumblr once ran the thought experiment of what would happen if you made a cake that was 44 percent vanilla extract. The conclusion was that it would be inedible. Tao cites the Tumblr post. That's the level of seriousness we're talking about.

OK. That's right now. What about the next phase?

The next phase is what he calls the red team era.

The terms come from cybersecurity. In cyber, the blue team is the team defending a system. The red team is the team attacking it, looking for vulnerabilities, probing for weaknesses. The whole point of having a red team is so that the blue team finds out about the holes BEFORE the actual bad guys do.

Tao wants to map that onto intellectual work. The blue team task is GENERATING things — new content, new structures, new arguments. The red team task is CHECKING things — verifying, testing, looking for flaws, suggesting improvements.

His claim is that AI is relatively safe on the red team. You give it your draft and ask, where are the weak points? Where might I be wrong? Did I miss a case? AI is genuinely good at this. It's pattern-matching against an enormous corpus of similar work and flagging things that look off. Worst case, it's wrong, and you ignore the suggestion. Best case, it catches something you would have missed.

AI on the blue team — generating the content in the first place — is much riskier. Because AI confabulates. It produces things that look right and aren't. And if it's generating something that no human or formal tool can then check, you have no defense.

So: AI as critic, safe. AI as creator of things nobody verifies, dangerous. Use it on the red team. Don't trust it on the blue team beyond what your red team can catch.

That's the proposal for the medium term. Critic, not creator.

And then we come to the long term. To the part that I think makes this whole essay sing.

Tao asks: what happens if the AI keeps getting better? What if, in some far-future scenario, the AI is just as capable as humans at every cognitive task we currently care about? What do we do then?

He walks through the obvious answers. None of them satisfy him.

You could retreat into pure formalism. Just say, look, all that matters is that the technical specifications are met. If the proof is correct, it's correct, regardless of who wrote it. That's clean. But it doesn't capture what it feels like to do mathematics or art. It doesn't tell you what to teach the next generation. It doesn't tell you which problems are worth working on.

You could retreat the other way. You could say, no, humans have some ineffable thing — a soul, a spark, an essential creativity — that AI will never have, no matter how impressive its outputs get. The trouble with that one, Tao notes gently, is that humans themselves are spiky and weird and sometimes succeed at things by what amounts to lucky word association and rote memorization. When we do it, we call it cognition. When the AI does it, we call it stochastic parroting. Hold that line too hard and you end up playing a god-of-the-gaps game, where the list of uniquely-human things keeps shrinking.

Or you could surrender. Say, AI will surpass us at everything, so why bother. Tao has a wonderful footnote here. For a vision of what this looks like in practice, he says, see the movie Wall-E.

None of those work. So what does?

Here's where he proposes something genuinely new. He calls it a Copernican view.

Back up. Long ago, humans thought the Earth was the center of the universe. Everything else orbited us. We were special. The cosmos was arranged around our home. This wasn't just astronomy; it was the emotional center of how people understood their place in things.

Then Copernicus, and Galileo, and the people who came after, slowly, painfully, dismantled that picture. The Earth rotates. The Earth orbits the Sun. The Sun orbits the center of the galaxy. The galaxy is part of an expanding universe with no center at all.

At first, this was devastating. We had been demoted. Our home wasn't special anymore. The universe wasn't paying attention to us.

But here's what we figured out, eventually. There's no actual contradiction between the universe being indifferent to Earth and us still loving Earth. We can still prioritize this planet. We can still care about its problems. We can still feel that our lives here matter. While ALSO accepting that other places exist, and would be just as important to whoever lived on them. The cosmic demotion didn't take away from our local commitment. It just put the local commitment in a bigger context.

Tao thinks the same move is now available, and possibly required, for thinking about minds.

For most of history, we have assumed that human intelligence is the center of the cognitive universe. The standard against which everything else is measured. The thing with special philosophical status.

We're now discovering, or maybe creating, other forms of intelligence. Comparable in some ways. Quite different in others. Not better. Not worse. Different planets of cognition.

The Copernican move is to stop arguing about which form of intelligence is the center. To accept that they all exist in the same category, with their own differences and complementarities. To still care more about the human form — that's where our lives are, where our meaning is — while accepting that other forms are also real, also part of the picture.

There's a precedent for how this can work. Chess. It has been decades since any human grandmaster could beat a chess engine. The engines are better, full stop. And chess didn't die. Chess transformed. Players use the engines to train, to revisit old theory, to probe the supposedly-invincible AI players for weaknesses, to invent new forms of competition where some moves are AI-assisted and some are not. The game survived. The meaning of the game evolved. And asking what the value of playing chess is still feels like a real question, just with a richer set of answers than it had a generation ago.

That's what Tao wants for the rest of us. Not the dystopia. Not the surrender. Not the desperate insistence on human exceptionalism. The Copernican move. Accept that there are other forms of intelligence now, that they have real capabilities, and figure out how to coexist in a way that lets each contribute what it's actually good at.

The essay closes on a careful, generous note. We have, the authors admit, already paid a real price for the chaotic way AI got released into the world. There's economic disruption. There's environmental cost. There are entire fields drowning in machine-generated noise. There are kids using AI to do their homework and never learning anything underneath. These aren't small problems.

But Tao thinks we haven't passed any tipping point yet. He thinks the genie is out of the bottle, sure, and it isn't going back in. But the smoke can still clear. The picture of what AI and humans together can be, once everyone has had a moment to think, hasn't been written yet.

And that's where the essay leaves you. Not with a prediction. Not with a prescription. With an invitation. Treat AI like vanilla, for now. Treat it like a red team, soon. Treat it like another planet of mind, eventually. Don't drink the vanilla. Don't trust the red team to write your blueprints. And don't forget that the other planets, however interesting, are not where you live.

You live here. With the humans. With the smell test. With the kind of proof that doesn't just verify a thing but tells you a story about it.

That's the part worth defending.
