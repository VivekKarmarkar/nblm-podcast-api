# Section 4 — AI and the nature of mathematical truth (pages 6-14)

This is the longest section in the paper and contains the most original technical thinking. It has 8 sub-sections. Below is a synthesis of the core argument plus key concrete examples.

## Load-bearing point

Mathematics has uniquely high standards of truth, but those standards in practice fall short of perfect rigor — human mathematicians rely on a "smell test" of intuition. AI changes everything about this: it can produce proofs that PASS formal verification but FAIL the smell test (no explanatory narrative), and conversely, formal verification can catch errors the smell test would miss. The right relationship is symbiotic, but figuring out the right balance is hard.

## Sub-section synthesis

**4.1 Mathematics and standards of proof.** Mathematics has, since Euclid and especially since the secure foundations of the early 20th century, the rare property that disputes can be RESOLVED. The Nelson 2011 example: a mathematician claimed the Peano Axioms were logically inconsistent, a subtle flaw was found, Nelson accepted it, withdrew the claim. But in practice, "arguments of human mathematicians fall short of the ideal of perfectly rigorous proof; minor and major mistakes in the literature are common." Some are corrected; others "passed on informally as part of the folklore."

**4.2 The Smell Test.** Mathematicians use heuristic "smell" — an aesthetic and intuitive sense — to assess whether a proof feels right before they read it carefully. Tao cites Aaronson's "Ten signs a claimed mathematical breakthrough is wrong" — there are common patterns of "bad smell" that experienced mathematicians detect before locating any specific logical flaw. One component of a "favorable smell" (per Thurston): the sense that an argument provides understanding or insight; that it shows not just THAT something is true but WHY — provides a "causal narrative" for the entailment, makes clear which parts of the argument do the "heavy lifting," which are novel, which are routine technical considerations.

**4.3 Formalization to the rescue?** Cutting-edge papers in some fields routinely exceed a hundred pages, making "line-by-line verification by human referees increasingly onerous." Formal proof assistants like Lean or Rocq can automatically check the validity of a proof if written in their precise computer language. Currently formalization takes "five to ten times longer than writing the proof in the first place" — but autoformalization (AI assisting to translate informal proofs to formal ones) could change that.

**4.4 Limitations of formal verification.** Two key problems:
1. Formal verification only certifies that the formal proof establishes the formal STATEMENT — but it cannot rule out errors in translation between the formal statement and the original intended statement. Example: Fermat's Last Theorem is about natural numbers, but does "natural numbers" start at 0 or 1? An AI could erroneously assume zero is allowed and produce a formally-certified proof that Fermat's Last Theorem is FALSE.
2. Even in pure math, only part of an argument is amenable to formalization. Around the deductive core there is a "penumbra of heuristic, empirical, or metamathematical reasoning" that gives motivation, intuition, suggests extensions. AI trained only on formal correctness could produce "odorless proofs" — superficially polished, even formally verified, but with no insight into the broader field. "In a world where all media generated is AI-polished to a high sheen, including mathematical proofs with beautiful typesetting and clear, GPT-produced explanations, is something lost in forsaking the grubbier, messier world of hand-written (or at least hand-typed) text?"

Also a startling footnote: it is theoretically possible to "hack" mathematics itself by subtly manipulating the formalization of key definitions in standard libraries like Mathlib.

**4.5 Adaptation to earlier challenges.** Math has done this before. Computer-assisted proofs like the four color theorem (1976) and the Kepler conjecture (1998) were initially controversial — impractical to fully check by hand. Over time, new standards developed: replicable code, isolating computational components in clear lemmas, providing checksums and sanity-check data. This shifted math standards toward those of natural science, where both theoretical argument and well-designed empirical experiment are accepted sources of truth.

**4.6 The evolution of AI-assisted mathematics.** Tao envisions a near future in which the BURDEN of producing verified deductive proofs falls increasingly on computers. The infamous phrases "the proof is left to the reader" or "by standard arguments, we have" would be replaced with calls to an LLM that produces both human-readable AND computer-verifiable justifications. Auto-formalization advances would let mathematicians easily explore how a result changes under different foundations of mathematics ("reverse mathematics" — which axioms do we actually need to prove this?). Human mathematicians may then shift focus to "softer" aspects: heuristics, motivation, experimental evidence, trial-and-error discovery processes — things less amenable to reinforcement learning because they are harder to verify and measure.

Also: an explicit warning about AlphaProof. Footnote 16 notes that proofs generated by AlphaProof for the 2024 International Mathematical Olympiad "contained numerous redundant or inexplicable steps but nevertheless were formally verified to be correct solutions." Aesthetic notions like "beauty" or "elegance" may become "even more decoupled than they currently are from the formal correctness of such arguments."

Negative risk: at the educational level, students "resort almost immediately to modern AI tools to perform their assigned coursework, achieving the immediate goal of producing verifiable answers to a given problem at the expense of developing more sustainable mathematical skills and intuition." At the research level: a "fourth paradigm" of data-driven mathematics could crowd out the more traditional paradigms — empirical evidence, theoretical reasoning, computational numerics — as well as the value mathematicians gain from visual, kinesthetic, physical, biological intuition. Worst case: "a flood of largely AI-generated papers containing results that are technically correct and new, but which do not contribute to broader mathematical narratives, and do not build up intuition for either the authors or the readers."

**4.7 Applying philosophical questions to real-world AI use.** Validity is only one component of value. There are nuanced value judgments about WHICH AI-generated findings are noteworthy, HOW they should be presented. Uncertainties in precedence and credit. Researchers should prioritize uses that benefit their field, that have cross-disciplinary benefits, that benefit humanity.

**4.8 Intellectual property and responsibility.** When AI is applied to a problem, who is responsible for errors? Who gets credit? Often the same party is not both. Most LLM training has been argued to fall under "Fair Use" by the developers. The authors run a thought experiment: would it be reasonable to claim fair use of all recorded knowledge if it meant saving the world from impending doom? From climate change? From ending all disease? From "merely" eradicating cancer?

Important new failure mode: "citogenesis" — circular citation loops. Tao describes how, in trying to use AI deep-research tools to systematically report literature on open math problems on a problem-tracking site, the deep-research tools then USED these reports as authoritative sources for their search — so adding the reports actually INTERFERED with future deep-research searches finding genuinely new literature. The increasing power of these tools necessitates "a more thorough vetting of the provenance of cited information."

## Why this section is the heart of the essay

Section 4 is where the math-specific argument gets really specific. The Fermat's-Last-Theorem-with-zero-allowed example, the AlphaProof "redundant inexplicable steps" example, the citogenesis example — these are CONCRETE, vivid, and surprising even to people who follow AI casually. They are also Tao at his best: a working mathematician describing specific failure modes he has personally seen in his own field.

For audio: this is where most of the SENSORY HOOKS live. The "smell test" is itself a beautiful sensory metaphor (Tao explicitly draws the analogy to "code smell" in software engineering). The contrast between a proof that PASSES formal verification but FAILS the smell test is a clean narrative beat. The citogenesis story is a self-contained mini-anecdote that lands well in spoken form.
