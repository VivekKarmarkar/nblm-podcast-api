# Section 1 — Introduction (pages 1-2)

## Load-bearing point

The opening establishes both AUTHORITY (Terence Tao, one of the most decorated living mathematicians, plus Tanya Klowden from the humanities side) and POSITIONING (the paper is not asking whether AI is good or bad — it is asking how humans should integrate it). The first line gets darkly comic: while writing this very paper "no less than three different digital agents inserted themselves into the narrative unsolicited" (verbatim, p.1). All of those AI "contributions" were promptly removed. This is the tone: a working mathematician who has been forced to think about AI because AI keeps showing up in his text editor.

## Three sub-pieces

**1.1 Definition of AI.** The authors deliberately use a broad definition: AI is "the broad spectrum of computer tools designed to perform increasingly complex cognitive tasks, including many that used to solely be the province of humans." They explicitly include both modern data-driven ML (LLMs, diffusion models) AND traditional good-old-fashioned AI (automated theorem provers, chess engines). This matters for the rest of the paper, because a lot of the math-specific argument turns on tools like Lean and Rocq, which are GOFAI, not LLMs.

**1.2 Purpose.** The authors note they come from "academic domains that are frequently viewed as polar opposites: mathematics and the study of art" — yet both find AI tools useful day-to-day, and both find "a surprising amount of common ground regarding the very messy, but universal, philosophical questions that real-world AI use poses." They argue twofold that AI tools should be developed and applied: they have the potential to "radically augment our natural human abilities" AND they are capable of "expanding what is possible beyond what we humans could do individually or within the limits of our own collective capacity."

**1.3 Faustian Bargain.** This is the dark turn. The "prisoner's dilemma" of market competition has pressured everyone to adopt AI "as hastily as possible, at the expense of more deliberate evaluation." Technology is "morally neutral" in principle but "exacerbates existing moral dilemmas, and creates new ones." Reference to WWII medical research on prisoners as analog for using ethically-murky training data. Key strategic observation: "there is no practical way to put the genie back in the bottle." Strict regulation now "would disproportionately shut down the more positive use cases of AI...without eliminating the more wasteful or malicious uses." So the conversation must move to MANAGING coexistence.

## What makes this section a great podcast hook

The "three AI agents inserted themselves into the narrative unsolicited" detail is gold — it is concrete, slightly absurd, immediately relatable to anyone who has used a modern IDE or word processor. It positions Tao as both insider (using these tools) and skeptic (manually deleting their unsolicited contributions). The Faustian Bargain framing is dramatic without being apocalyptic. The genie-in-the-bottle observation is the philosophical pivot the whole essay turns on.
