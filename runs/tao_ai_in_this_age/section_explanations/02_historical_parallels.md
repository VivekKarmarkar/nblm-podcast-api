# Section 2 — Historical parallels: is this time different? (pages 3-4)

## Load-bearing point

The authors directly confront the "AI is just another tool, like the printing press" argument — and refute it. The crux: previous automation affected the COMMUNICATION of intellectual work; modern AI affects the CREATION of intellectual work. That is a categorical difference.

## Two sub-pieces

**2.1 Past integration.** Past technologies (the internet, scientific computing, LaTeX) underwent "phase transitions" in scientific practice — broad and rapid switchovers. But these affected "secondary aspects of the profession, such as the communication and dissemination of results rather than the creation of such results." The printing press revolutionized information flow but "did not significantly alter the understanding of what an idea or a piece of information was." The original generation of content was still done by deliberate human actions.

**2.2 Modern AI.** Modern AI "can automate large portions of the creative process itself, enabling the mass-generation of intellectual products, such as artwork, mathematical proofs, or scientific or philosophical theories, with far less human oversight than was previously required." This creates "an unprecedented decoupling between the outward form of such products, and the values and thought processes used to create these products."

Example: a diffusion model can produce an aesthetically pleasing landscape that "was not directly inspired by any particular location in the physical world." The aesthetic response becomes decoupled from the original sources of such aesthetics.

The authors note this is not new philosophical territory — Searle's Chinese Room thought experiment from 1980, the "AI effect" where chess used to be a marker of intelligence until engines beat humans and then it wasn't anymore, and the Turing test which has now been "effectively passed by modern LLMs."

The implicit question: when the markers of intelligence keep moving as machines acquire each one, do we have to keep moving the goalposts, or do we need a completely different framing of what intelligence is?

## What makes this section narratively useful

The printing-press analogy + immediate rebuttal is a clean two-beat structure that lands well in audio. The chess-test observation — that we used to call chess intelligence until chess engines stopped being impressive, then quietly stopped calling it intelligence — is a punchy concrete example of how humans deal with this discomfort. The diffusion-model landscape that wasn't inspired by anything is a vivid, easily-grasped example.
