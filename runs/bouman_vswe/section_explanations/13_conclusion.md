# 13 — Conclusion

## What they did
Introduced VSWE for estimating subsurface geometric and mechanical properties from visible surface waves. Method: extract spatial and temporal frequency info from surface waves in a video → represent as a dispersion relation → use a physics-based approach to solve for the properties that explain the wave behavior.

## What they validated
- Simulated data: sensitive to small (5%) changes in tissue parameters.
- Real gelatin phantoms: thickness within caliper confidence interval; stiffness within 1.2% of rheometry.
- Simulated 3D human leg: recovers spatially-varying thickness from a sliding observation window.

## The closing line
"Our work shows the potential of everyday visual data to reveal critical information deep beneath the skin."

## Conversational close
This is the moment for the broader vision callout. The paper itself is restrained. The podcast can be slightly less restrained: a future where checking your tissue health is as casual as recording a video of your calf. A future where structural inspection of bridges or buildings doesn't need specialized equipment. Where the camera in your phone, combined with the right software, is its own non-destructive testing lab.

The VVT-to-VSWE arc lands here: VVT was the first proof that videos can see inside objects. VSWE generalizes the idea to surface waves, which means you don't need to model the whole object — just a section. That's how this scales from labs to fields, from cubes to legs, from finite phantoms to potentially infinite structures.
