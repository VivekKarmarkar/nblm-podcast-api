# Section 5 — The costs and benefits of AI (pages 15-19)

## Load-bearing point

This section zooms OUT from mathematics to broader society. It tallies the costs of AI honestly — economic displacement, environmental footprint, the digital divide — and argues that despite these the case for AI is not lost; it is a case for HARM REDUCTION rather than abolition or laissez-faire.

## Four sub-pieces

**5.1 Economic and societal impacts: who benefits?** The authors propose that AI development should "prioritize benefit for humanity as a whole" and that AI applications should "remain directly useful to humans (individually or collectively)." For each use case, ask: who benefits? Will it benefit society or just deliver cost savings to a small group? "Extreme economic gain for a small number of individuals at the cost of millions comes at an intolerable and unacceptable moral cost."

Historical anchor: the Nottingham textile workers of the early 19th century — the original Luddites — were not anti-progress in any blanket sense; they were workers facing existential threats to their livelihoods in a harsh economic climate. The Industrial Revolution did benefit society in the long run, but it came with "real, measurable human costs." Today, "unlike in the Luddites' time, we are already seeing skilled workers replaced not with lower-wage human labor, but with AI." Entry-level jobs that used to be the path to prosperity for new workers "simply vanish, opportunity vanishes with them."

The current business climate seeks a "Wunderwaffe" — a miracle weapon — optimized for power and impact in the hope it will outrun any potential problems. But failure to quantify the human cost does "a great disservice to humanity as a whole for the benefit of a select few." The natural reaction to imposed technology is rejection — "throwing the baby (AI uses that offer quantifiable benefits) out with the bathwater."

**5.2 Tallying the costs of AI.** AI infrastructure cannot be built as a hobby like the 1970s computing revolution. There is no garage of parts a single Steve-Jobs-like figure can build into an empire. A better analogy: the transcontinental railroad of the late 19th century. Companies had to build a fleet of massive engines and lay thousands of miles of rail before any train could quickly transport goods. This huge upfront capital cost creates a "new class of technological elites" who control enormous sums of capital and finite resources (land, energy, water, skilled labor) — "just as the robber barons of the nineteenth century Gilded Age did."

Environmental cost: heavy energy and water consumption. AI itself is sometimes proposed as a solution to climate change. Marginal costs may decline as infrastructure scales. But "to date, none of the large AI models in operation have provided a solution to even offset their own resource consumption and waste emissions."

Important technical risk: modern AI tools do not pursue truth through "manifestation in the physical world" — they rely on human-generated data, with the result that there is a "clear limit to how much AI can be used to generate new information in a domain before AI collapse becomes a serious problem." Without enough genuine human-generated content, AI "becomes ungrounded from reality." Mathematics, with its formal verification, "may have a tolerance for higher levels of AI contamination than other domains; but...it is not completely immune to this danger."

**5.3 The Digital Divide.** Large-scale AI tools may only be available to "well-financed or well-connected research groups, or to individuals who are the most willing to hand over their personal data and look past any ethical concerns." This creates a divide between "the AI-have and the AI-have-nots."

A subtler second digital divide: when dominant AI models are capitalized, privatized, competing for finite resources, they are incentivized to develop "spiky" capabilities to retain competitive advantage — rather than consistent performance across domains. As users get locked into one model, one model may give meaningful advantage in a particular research area, creating divisions even WITHIN the group that has reliable AI access.

Counterpoint: many AI benefits in research do not require the most advanced models. "Smaller local models, as well as non-LLM technologies such as proof assistants, demonstrate the capacity to return meaningful results faster and more efficiently." A "diverse array of smaller, more targeted models, maintained by a community of users" could emerge as a viable alternative. Industry practitioners and policymakers have called for regulatory action; the authors suggest investing in "a national or multi-national public-facing coalition for advanced AI research and the development of a large, publicly funded and publicly accessible AI resource."

**5.4 Harm Reduction.** Analogy: aviation. In the early days, plane travel was "incredibly unsafe...with countless horrific accidents." Today it is the safest long-distance transportation. To follow a similar trajectory (hopefully with fewer fatal incidents) AI will require "decisive actions to reduce harm." Best practices must be defined; training and regulation designed to enhance responsible uses and discourage concealed or harmful ones.

But this is "a fine needle to thread." A responsible AI user can be overtaken in the short term by less scrupulous rivals using faster, more unreliable AI to accelerate their work. Or they can be "derided, condemned, and excluded by cohorts of AI-distrusting peers" for even considering the technology. The "current, largely laissez faire approach...does not seem promising."

Wikipedia is offered as a partial precedent. In the early 2000s Wikipedia disrupted education; many instructors banned it, students just copy-pasted from it. Over time a consensus emerged: use it as a STARTING POINT, follow the secondary sources, check against independent ones. Today Wikipedia is accepted as a useful academic resource. "Could we reach a similar level of responsible acceptance with AI? We are cautiously optimistic that this is possible; but it will require sustained effort and clear philosophical guidance."

## What makes this section narratively important

Section 5 anchors the essay in real human stakes — the Luddites, the railroad robber-barons, the water-and-energy consumption, the digital divide. It also offers REASONABLE concrete suggestions (smaller community-driven local models, publicly funded AI resources, Wikipedia-like norms of use). For audio, the Luddites-weren't-anti-progress beat is a great myth-busting moment. The railroad/robber-baron analogy lands well. The aviation analogy works as a hopeful counterweight.
